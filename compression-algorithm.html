Compression dosage — Australia

Class 1 · Mild
18–21 mmHg
Tired/aching legs, mild oedema, DVT travel prophylaxis, early varicose veins. Double-layer Tubigrip (~15–20 mmHg).
Class 2 · Moderate
23–32 mmHg
Active VLU, moderate oedema, post-sclerotherapy, healed venous ulcer maintenance. Primary clinical target for most CVI.
Class 3 · Strong
34–46 mmHg
Severe VLU, chronic venous insufficiency, lymphoedema. Standard 4-layer targets 40 mmHg.
Class 4 · Very Strong
>49 mmHg
Severe lymphoedema, elephantiasis, large limb de-bulking. Usually requires custom fitting.

Graduation rule Pressure must always be highest at the ankle, decreasing up the limb. Never apply equal or reverse-graduated compression.

Clinical decision pathway

1
Obtain validated Doppler ABPI
Do not rely on palpation alone before initiating Class 2+ compression.
2
Classify wound and oedema
Determine wound type (venous/arterial/mixed/lymphoedema), oedema grade, and exudate level.
3
Select compression class
ABPI ≥ 0.9 → full compression by clinical indication. ABPI 0.8–0.9 → Class 1 only. ABPI < 0.8 → medical review required before any compression.
4
Choose appropriate product
Inelastic/short-stretch for ambulatory lymphoedema; elastic or multi-layer for venous ulcers.
5
Reassess regularly
Repeat ABPI and wound review every 12 weeks minimum, or sooner if clinical deterioration occurs.

Elastic vs inelastic — when to choose

Elastic (long-stretch)
High resting pressure. Suited for ambulatory and non-ambulatory patients. Most VLU bandage systems. Examples: Profore, Coban 2, K-Two.
Inelastic (short-stretch)
High working pressure, low resting pressure. Ideal for ambulatory lymphoedema. Massaging effect via calf pump. Examples: Comprilan, Putterbinde, Rosidal K.
Australian clinical standard For active venous leg ulcers, the primary target is RAL Class 2 or 3 (23–46 mmHg). Multi-layer systems achieving sustained graduated pressure are preferred over single-layer hosiery.

Double-layer application

1
Measure and cut
Measure base of toes to just below knee. Cut double that length plus 2–3 cm overlap.
2
Apply first layer
Slide the first half onto the limb from toes to below the knee.
3
Fold back — second layer
Fold the remainder back over. The outer layer must end 2–3 cm higher than the inner to prevent a pressure "ring".
In-between rule If measurement falls exactly on a border (e.g. 24 cm), choose the larger size — especially if ABPI is borderline (0.8–0.9).
Joint flexion When applying over the knee, measure and apply with the leg slightly bent to prevent bunching in the popliteal fossa.
⚠ Latex warning Standard Tubigrip contains natural rubber latex. Use Tubigrip LF or Medigrip LF for patients with a latex allergy.
Australian practice The RAL GZ 387 (German) standard is most commonly used for medical-grade therapy. BS 6612 (British) is preferred for elderly or fragile skin. AFNOR (French) appears in some oncology settings.

Patient presses firmly on the big toenail for 5 seconds until white, then releases.

2

✓
Pass: Pink returns in <3 seconds — adequate circulation.
✗
Fail: Stays white or >3 seconds — may be too tight. Remove and call clinician.

Daily care — do's and don'ts

Do
Check skin at bandage edges for redness or "cutting in".
Elevate legs above heart when sitting.
Keep bandage dry — waterproof cover for showering.
Stay active — gentle walking helps the calf pump.
Don't
Fold or roll down the bandage top — creates a pressure ring.
Wear compression while sleeping unless specifically advised.
Patch or cut the bandage yourself if it feels tight.
Ignore heaviness or throbbing that worsens over time.
